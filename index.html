01 / GLOBAL MIX

The world still runs on fossil fuels.

In 2024, despite a decade of headlines about the energy transition, fossil fuels still produced nearly 60% of global electricity. The shift is real, but slow. Here's what the world's grid actually looks like.

Coal still leads. Solar grows fastest.

Coal has been the largest single source of electricity for over 50 years. In 2024 it generated 35% of global power. Natural gas holds a steady second place at 22%, a position it has held for two decades.

The interesting story is at the bottom. Solar PV grew 33% year-over-year. Wind grew 10%. Renewables together provided one-third of global electricity. For the first time, renewables plus nuclear together covered two-fifths of global generation.

The transition reality

The IEA projects that in 2025, renewables will overtake coal as the largest source of electricity globally. In 2026, solar and wind each individually surpass nuclear. The transition is real, but coal will still be a major source for years.

03 / PRICE FORMATION

How energy prices actually move.

Energy markets are uniquely sensitive to supply shocks because demand is inelastic in the short term. You can't suddenly stop driving or heating your home. So when supply drops, prices spike before demand can adjust. Pick a scenario to see how.

04 / THE NEW DEMAND

Data centers are rewriting demand.

For 50 years, electricity demand growth came from industry, transportation electrification, and air conditioning. AI has added a new driver. Data centers used 415 TWh of electricity in 2024 (1.5% of global consumption). By 2030 the IEA projects 945 TWh, equivalent to Japan's total annual electricity use.

Projected growth 2024–2030

Data center electricity consumption is set to more than double in six years. The US and China together account for nearly 80% of the projected growth. In the United States, data centers will consume more electricity for processing data than for the production of aluminum, steel, cement, chemicals, and all energy-intensive manufacturing combined by 2030.

05 / WHAT IT MEANS

Five things worth holding onto.

After a 1.5 hour lecture, four hours of building this, and pulling data from IEA, IMF, EIA, and Lazard, these are the macro forces I'd bet on shaping the next decade of energy markets.

01

The transition is real but slow

Fossil fuels still produced nearly 60% of global electricity in 2024. Solar and wind are growing fast, but coal will be a major source for at least another decade.

02

Production cost defines power

Saudi Arabia produces oil for under $10 a barrel. The US shale industry needs $55+. This cost gap is why OPEC+ can dictate prices and why every oil shock reshuffles global politics.

03

Demand is inelastic

You cannot stop driving overnight. You cannot stop heating your home. Short-term energy demand barely responds to price, which is why supply shocks cause price spikes, not gradual adjustments.

04

AI is the new oil demand

Data centers will add roughly 530 TWh of demand by 2030. That is the equivalent of adding another Japan to the global grid. Power markets, not chip capacity, will be the constraint on AI scaling.

05

Geopolitics is the wildcard

The 2026 Iran tensions briefly pushed Brent above $75. A real Strait of Hormuz disruption could move prices 50% in a week. Energy markets stay cheap exactly until they don't.
